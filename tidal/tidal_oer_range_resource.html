Tidal range resource¶

Let us try to place this energy into context. The maximum theoretical potential energy available from a head difference (or simply, an elevation difference) \(H\) (m) in Joules is given by the classic study of [Pra84] as:

(1)¶\[E_{max} = \frac{1}{2} \rho g A H^2,\]

where \(\rho\) is the fluid density (kg/m3), \(g\) is the gravitational acceleration (m/s2) and \(A\) is the impounded plan surface area (m2).

If we consider \(H\) to be the tidal range, the above equation can give us an indication of how much energy is available for extraction. The resource available increases linearly with the impounded area \(A\). However the resource is amplified much more when \(H\) increases through a quadratic relationship. Using the above equation, we can calculate how much tidal energy is encompassed in each transition of the tide, as in Figure 1 below.

Figure 1: Calculation of potential energy. \(E_{\text{flood}}\) is the potential energy in the transition during a flood tide from low water, to high water, and \(E_{\text{ebb}}\)

is the potential energy in the transition during an ebb tide from high water, to low water respectively.

The tidal range at a given location does not remain constant. In fact, the tidal elevation at any point is the result of many tidal waves that add up to the overall wave developed [Par07]. During spring tides, the principal tide driven by the moon forces (\(M_2\)) constructively adds up leading to a greater tidal range, whereas during neap tides the opposite occurs.

By extending the rationale of Figure 1 and equation (1) in a realistic sea elevation signal that considers these variations in time we can proceed and quantify how the tidal range energy varies at any point. The Figure is produced for a location at Swansea Bay, UK.

Figure 2: Evolution of (a) tidal elevations, (b) tidal range and (c) the theoretical tidal range energy (as calculated using Eq. 1 normalised by unit impounded surface area for a site near Swansea Bay in the Bristol Channel, UK. The values \(\eta_{\text{crest}}\) and \(\eta_{\text{trough}}\) represent the tidal wave crest (high water) and trough (low water) in each tidal period, while \(H_{\text{max}}\) the maximum head difference that could be exploited in each transition from crest to trough and vice-versa. The tidal signal considered here includes eight tide constituents and begins on 06/05/2003.
